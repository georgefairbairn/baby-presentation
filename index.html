🤔 What is the first sense a baby develops in the womb?

A) Hearing
B) Sight
C) Touch
D) Taste
Bonus facts: Touch develops at week 8 and the baby is sensitive across their whole body by week 16. Hearing develops at week 18 — they can hear you! Sight is the last sense to fully develop, even after birth.

🚫 Nutrition: What to Avoid

  • 🍷 Alcohol — none is the safest amount
  • 🐟 High-mercury fish (swordfish, shark, marlin)
  • 🧀 Soft/unpasteurised cheeses — brie, camembert, stilton
  • 🥕 Raw/undercooked meat, eggs & sushi
  • ☕ Caffeine under 200mg/day (1 coffee or 2 builder's teas)
  • Deli meats & pâté — yes, even from M&S

🇨🇦 Navigating Healthcare in Canada

  • Finding a family doctor or OB-GYN — not automatic like the NHS
  • Midwife vs OB-GYN: both are valid paths, midwives offer more continuity
  • Provincial health insurance covers most prenatal care
  • Some extras are paid — ultrasound clinics, genetic testing
Typical appointment schedule: Monthly until week 28, then every 2 weeks until week 36, then weekly until birth.

📞 When to Call Your Doctor

🚨 Call immediately: Heavy bleeding • Severe abdominal pain • Baby stops moving (T3) • Preeclampsia signs (sudden swelling, severe headaches, vision changes) • High fever • Cramping with bleeding (T1)
🟠 Call within 24 hours: Reduced fetal movement (T3) • Painful urination • Persistent vomiting • Unusual discharge
🟢 Mention at next appointment: Mild spotting (T1) • Braxton Hicks • General aches, swelling & heartburn

Things That Can Go Wrong

Not to scare you — to prepare you

Miscarriage

1 in 5 known pregnancies, 80% in first 12 weeks. Mostly chromosomal — not anything you did.

Ectopic

1 in 80 pregnancies. Signs: sharp one-sided pain, shoulder tip pain, dizziness. Needs immediate attention.

Preeclampsia

After week 20. Sudden swelling, severe headaches, vision changes. More common in first pregnancies.

Gestational Diabetes

Mid-second trimester, often no symptoms. Picked up by routine screening. Manageable.

📝 Before You Conceive

  • Start folic acid 400mcg/day — ideally 3 months before trying
  • Both partners aim for healthy weight
  • Cut alcohol and smoking — both partners
  • Get a preconception checkup
  • Review any medications with your doctor
  • Get vaccinations up to date
  • Track your cycle to understand your fertile window

🩺 The Preconception Checkup

For Her

  • Underlying conditions check
  • Medication review
  • Immunity to rubella & chickenpox
  • Cervical screening
  • Family history of genetic conditions

For Him

  • General health & sperm health
  • Medication review
  • Family history
Why it matters: Easier to address issues before pregnancy. Establishes a relationship with your Canadian doctor before you urgently need one.

💪 Staying Healthy During Pregnancy

  • Attend all prenatal appointments
  • Stay hydrated
  • Keep moving — walking, swimming, prenatal yoga
  • 150 mins moderate activity per week
  • Rest when needed, especially in T1
  • Manage stress — this is a marathon, not a sprint

⛔ Things to Avoid During Pregnancy

  • 🍷 Alcohol
  • ♨️ Hot tubs & saunas
  • 🐱 Cat litter (toxoplasmosis)
  • Certain cleaning products & pesticides
  • ✈️ Long-haul flights in T3 without checking with doctor
  • Contact sports, heavy lifting & fall risks

💜 Things Outside Your Control

  • Chromosomal abnormalities cause ~50% of miscarriages — happen by chance, not your fault
  • 80% occur in first 12 weeks — risk at 12 wks is 1.7%, by 16 wks just 0.5%
  • Risk drops significantly once heartbeat is detected
  • 85% go on to have a healthy pregnancy after miscarriage
  • Even after two or three, 75% still succeed
Bottom line: Focus on what's in your control. Be kind to yourselves about what isn't.

Part 2: Birth & Hospital

🤔 How long is the average first-time labour?

A) 4–6 hours
B) 8–10 hours
C) 12–18 hours
D) 24+ hours
Details: Active labour is typically 4–8 hours of that total. Early labour is best spent at home. Second labours are usually significantly shorter. 12–18 hours is an average — both shorter and longer are completely normal.

Stage 1: Early Labour

  • Contractions begin — irregular, building to regular
  • Cervix dilates 0–6cm
  • The longest stage — best spent at home
  • Rest, eat, bath, distract yourself
  • Try to sleep if it starts at night
📣 The 5-1-1 Rule: Contractions 5 minutes apart, lasting 1 minute, for 1 hour — then it's time to go.

Stage 2: Active Labour

  • Cervix dilates 6–10cm
  • Contractions stronger, longer & closer together
  • Pain relief decisions happen now
  • Pushing begins at full dilation
  • Pushing lasts 20 mins to 2 hours for first-timers
  • Your care team will guide you throughout

Stage 3: Delivery of the Placenta

  • Happens after birth — surprises most first-timers!
  • Usually within 30 minutes
  • Two options: managed (injection) or physiological (natural)
  • Usually discussed in your birth plan
  • Much easier than what just happened 😅

🚘 Signs It's Time to Go to Hospital

Go now: 5-1-1 rule met • Waters break • Heavy bleeding • Reduced baby movement • Trust your instincts
👨 Partner notes: Hospital number saved in phone. Know the route. Don't wait if in doubt — they'd rather send you home than have you leave it too late.

💊 Pain Relief Options in Canada

  • Epidural — most effective, anaesthesiologist, most hospitals
  • Nitrous oxide (laughing gas) — widely available
  • IV opioids (morphine/fentanyl) — takes the edge off
  • Water therapy — bath/shower, good for early labour
  • TENS machine — best early, hire in advance
  • Going natural — breathing, movement, massage, hypnobirthing
You can change your mind at any time. Not all options available at all stages.

👨 Partner's Guide: Your Job on the Day

  • Before leaving: Bag packed, route known, number saved
  • Early labour: Keep her comfortable, time contractions, stay calm
  • At hospital: Be her advocate, know the birth plan
  • Active labour: Be present. No phone. Comfort — hand rub, back rub, cold flannel
  • After birth: Skin-to-skin if needed, cut cord, take photos
Honest advice: You'll feel helpless — that's normal. Be calm and present. Eat something. It's ok to be overwhelmed, just don't show it 😄

✨ The Golden Hour

The first hour after birth — protect it

  • Skin-to-skin: Regulates baby's temp, heart rate & breathing. Kickstarts bonding.
  • First breastfeed ideally within the first hour
  • Delayed cord clamping if chosen
  • Partner can do skin-to-skin if needed
  • Mum will likely be shaking — totally normal, just adrenaline
  • Keep the room calm, visitors minimal

🤰 What Happens to Mum After Birth

  • Placenta delivery (Stage 3)
  • Stitches if needed — local anaesthetic
  • Vitals monitored for a couple of hours
  • Take the pain relief offered — seriously, take it
  • First meal — pack good snacks 🥪
  • First bathroom trip — ask for help
  • Maternity pads: your new best friend for weeks

👶 What Happens to Baby After Birth

  • Apgar score at 1 & 5 mins — heart rate, breathing, colour, tone, reflexes (out of 10, 7+ is normal)
  • Weighed and measured
  • Vitamin K injection — important for blood clotting, highly recommended
  • Newborn hearing screening — quick and painless
  • Identity bands fitted
  • Baby stays with you unless there's a medical reason

🏠 Going Home

  • Vaginal birth: discharged 24–48 hours
  • C-section: 2–4 days
  • Checks before leaving: bleeding controlled, passed urine, baby feeding, meconium passed
  • Public health nurse visits at home — standard in most Canadian provinces
🚗 Cannot leave without a correctly fitted car seat — they will check!

Part 3: First Year

🤔 How many hours do newborns sleep per day?

A) 8–10 hours
B) 12–14 hours
C) 16–18 hours
D) 20+ hours
The catch: They sleep in 2–3 hour chunks, day and night. Longest stretch is typically 3–4 hours. Many start consolidating at 3–4 months. “Sleeping through” officially means just 5–6 hours.

🤔 How many nappy changes in the first year?

A) ~1,000
B) ~1,500
C) ~2,500
D) ~4,000
Fun facts: 6–8 changes/day early on. They can dirty a nappy immediately after you change them. Meconium (first few days) is dark and sticky. Transitions to yellow by week 2. Stock up in advance!

💦 Newborn Survival Guide: First 2 Weeks

  • Complete shell shock is normal
  • Feed every 2–3 hours — including nights
  • Wind after every feed
  • Umbilical cord stump — keep dry, falls off in 1–3 weeks
  • Sponge baths only until the stump falls off
  • Accept every offer of help
  • Lower your standards for everything except baby care
  • Talk to each other 💕

🥚 Breastfeeding

  • Recommended for 6 months by Health Canada
  • Doesn't always come naturally — that's not failure
  • Cluster feeding is real — and exhausting
  • Know the signs: nipple soreness, engorgement, mastitis
  • Lactation consultants available across Canada
  • Burp after every feed
  • Growth spurts = increased demand, not something wrong

🍼 Formula & Combination Feeding

  • Formula is completely valid — fed is best, no judgement
  • Follow prep instructions carefully — sterilisation matters
  • ~$150–200 CAD/month
  • Combination feeding works well — useful if mum needs a break
  • Feeding shouldn't be painful after the first week — get help if it is

😴 Safe Sleeping Guidelines

  • Always on their back
  • Firm, flat mattress — no loose bedding, pillows or bumpers
  • Own crib in your room for at least 6 months
  • Room temperature 18–20°C
  • Never co-sleep on a sofa or armchair

🌙 Surviving the Nights

  • No circadian rhythm early on — they don't know it's night
  • Tag team with your partner
  • Sleep when baby sleeps — yes, everyone says it, but try
  • Swaddling helps many newborns
  • White noise is surprisingly effective
  • Differentiate day and night — bright/chatty vs dark/quiet
  • Loose routine from 6–8 weeks: bath → feed → sleep

🌞 When Does It Get Better?

  • Longer stretches at 3–4 months
  • Most sleeping through by 6–12 months
  • Sleep regressions at 4, 8 & 12 months — just when you think you've cracked it!
💡 Every baby is different. Comparison is the thief of sanity.

Milestones: Months 0–2

👶 Newborn → 2 Months

  • Focuses on faces — yours is their favourite
  • Responds to sound
  • First smile at 6–8 weeks 😊
  • Lifts head briefly during tummy time

Milestones: Months 3–4

👶 3–4 Months

  • Laughs and coos 😂
  • Tracks moving objects with eyes
  • Reaches and grabs for things
  • Sleep may start to consolidate

Milestones: Months 5–6

👶 5–6 Months

  • Rolls over (front to back first)
  • Sits with support
  • Solid foods start at ~6 months 🍽
  • Recognises familiar faces and voices

Milestones: Months 7–9

👶 7–9 Months

  • Sits independently
  • Crawling (some babies skip this entirely!)
  • Separation anxiety begins
  • Babbling — mamama, dadada 💕

Milestones: Months 10–12

👶 10–12 Months

  • Pulls to standing, cruising along furniture
  • Pincer grip — picks up small things
  • First words may appear
  • First steps around 12 months (9–18 months is normal range)

📞 Doctor vs. Normal (Baby Edition)

🚨 Call immediately: High fever (38°C under 3 months) • Breathing difficulty • Seizures • Unresponsive • Blood in stool/vomit • Dehydration signs
🟠 Call within 24 hours: Persistent fever • Vomiting/diarrhoea • Rash • Not feeding well • Unusual crying
🟢 Totally normal: Hiccups • Sneezing • Noisy breathing • Spotty skin in first weeks • Crying for no reason • Fontanelle pulsing

💕 Looking After Your Relationship

  • The first year is hard on relationships — knowing that helps
  • You'll both be exhausted — be patient with each other
  • Arguments about nothing = sleep deprivation talking
  • Divide responsibilities clearly
  • Check in with each other daily
  • Take shifts — protect each other's rest
  • Accept help — not the time for pride

💜 Don't Forget Each Other

  • Make time for small moments together
  • Physical recovery takes time — be patient and communicate
  • Talk about how you're both feeling
  • Bottling it up always makes it worse

💚 Postnatal Depression

1 in 5 mums • 1 in 10 dads
  • Baby blues (first 2 weeks) vs PND — they're different
  • Signs: persistent sadness, anxiety, feeling detached from baby, inability to cope
  • Speak to your doctor — it's common, treatable, and nothing to be ashamed of

🤔 How much should you have saved before baby arrives?

A) $5,000–$10,000
B) $10,000–$15,000
C) $20,000–$25,000
D) $30,000+
Breakdown: Emergency fund (3–6 months expenses), one-time setup ($1K–$5K), income buffer for parental leave (48% income loss). Vancouver pushes toward the top end. Open an RESP immediately — government matches 20% of first $2,500/year.

💲 What is EI & Do You Qualify?

  • EI replaces income during maternity/parental leave
  • Need 600 insurable hours in past 52 weeks (~4 months full-time)
  • Replaces 55% of average insurable weekly earnings
  • 2026 maximum: $729/week (max insurable earnings $68,900/year)
  • Benefits are taxable
⚠️ Apply as soon as you stop working. Waiting more than 4 weeks may mean losing benefits.

🤰 Maternity Leave

  • 15 weeks — birth parent only, cannot be shared
  • Can start up to 12 weeks before due date
  • Must end no later than 17 weeks after due date or birth
  • Paid at 55%, up to $729/week in 2026
  • Apply for maternity and parental at the same time — saves doing it twice

👪 Parental Leave Options

Can be shared between both parents. Both must choose the same option.

Standard

  • Up to 35 weeks per parent
  • Max 40 weeks shared
  • 55% up to $729/week

Extended

  • Up to 61 weeks per parent
  • Max 69 weeks shared
  • 33% up to $437/week
Use-it-or-lose-it: 5 weeks (standard) or 8 weeks (extended) reserved exclusively for the second parent.

💡 Making the Most of Your Leave

  • Cannot switch between standard and extended once payments start
  • Both parents must choose the same option before applying
  • Apply as soon as you stop working
  • Check with HR — many Vancouver employers top up EI
  • Second parent's use-it-or-lose-it weeks are genuinely worth taking
  • Canada Child Benefit kicks in at birth — apply immediately (tax-free monthly income)

💵 Money-Saving Tips

  • Buy second-hand where safe — Facebook Marketplace, Kijiji, Once Upon A Child
  • Never second-hand: car seats & cot mattresses
  • Accept every hand-me-down offered
  • Buy nappies in bulk — Costco pays for itself
  • Don't over-buy clothing — they grow impossibly fast, especially first 6 months
